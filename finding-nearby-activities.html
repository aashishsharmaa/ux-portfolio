Case Study 01 / Expedia, Local Experts, 2019-2020

Finding nearby activities for in-trip travelers

A map view to help travelers orient themselves in a new city, and discover activities near them.

Context

Existing design of a list view was not helpful for in-trip travelers.

In-trip travelers book flights and hotels before traveling, but decide on the activities they want to do when they reach the destination.

Studies done by the UX Research team had revealed that in-trip travelers were hard-pressed for time, and wanted to find an activity near them.

The existing booking flow used a list view to show the activities searched by a user.

Hypothesis

Travelers will buy more when they can see what's near them

Analytics had shown that in-trip users had nearly 80% of searches and 50% of bookings on the app.

All of the competitors used a map view. And within Expedia, the Hotels team also used a map view to boost their sales.

Data supporting a map view.

So, our team was confident in the value of this user need, leading to the hypothesis that ...

"If travelers can visually see activities around them, then it will be easier to decide on an activity, and they will buy more."

Hypothesis for a map view.

Stakeholders & Role

A remote squad team in 3 time zones

I was part of a squad team, spread across Australia, India, and USA, comprising a Product Manager, a TPM, and Software Developers, and myself as a UX Designer.

'Things to Do' team is internally known as Local Experts (LX).

Outside of the squad, we had the advantage of seeking help from a User Researcher, a Content Strategist, and a Data Scientist.

As a UX Designer, I was responsible for

  • Understanding the business case and the user need.
  • Studying how competitors had tried to solve this need.
  • Taking over the work done by a previous designer.
  • Incrementally, designing solutions to enhance the end-user experience.
  • Leveraging Expedia's design system for cohesive development.

Measuring user behaviour

Issues in development

The curious case of an 'Unbreakable Pin'

I took over the designs made by the previous designer on the team. These were similar to the designs done by the Hotels team and used a cluster pin behavior.

When a user would tap on a cluster pin, it would break down into multiple pins at a zoomed-in level.

The cluster pin was a component within the company-wide design system.

Cluster pin was designed to break down to multiple pins at a zoomed-in level.

Use of real data to populate the map pins resulted in two problems:

  1. There were a lot of zoom issues.
  2. Some cluster pins wouldn't break further.

Why won't the pin break?

Data showed that nearly 35% of pins did not break further. It happened because a single supplier could supply multiple activities from a single location.

Hotels team did not face this issue as there is only one hotel at a location. It was not the case with activities.

When real data was used, some pins did not break further.

To solve for this interaction problem, I sketched out and then detailed, a few possible options:

#1 - Bottom sheet, with 2nd card partially visible, scrolls over the map view as a list view.

#2 - Bottom sheet for single activity pin, and a horizontal carousel for multiple activities.

#3 - Horizontal carousel for all types of pins.

#4 - Horizontal carousel for all types of pins, and for multiple activities an option to see all activities in a list view.

Since this was close to launch, we agreed to use option (#3) a horizontal carousel for all pins, as it was available in the design system, and could be readily used by developers. Other options were not in the design system and needed more development time.

Interestingly, post launch analytics showed that most users did not go past 3 cards.

And in a user study done by the UXR team, users requested to see a list view after first few cards. So option (#4) was what users needed.

Horizontal carousel was used for MVP.

Launch of MVP

We launched with a positive impact.

Though the engagement with the map button was low (~3%), the conversion was nearly double for map users (~8%) than the list view users (~4%).

Overall, we saw an increase of 11% in orders, 3% in CVR, and 6% in Gross Purchase, validating the hypothesis for the need of a map view.

Conversion of map view was double of list view.

Usage data validated the hypothesis.

Learnings from MVP

Insights for further enhancements

Usage data & a user study done post-launch gave insights for further enhancements.

Insights for further enhancements.

Enhancement #1

Making maps more discoverable

In MVP, we had a small entry to map view.

To increase prominence of Map on Search Results page, we added a mini-map above the search listings.

In case users would not tap on Map and would scroll down, I proposed using a slim card which would appear when a user scrolls down. The intent was to show the benefits of using a map view.

We had a small entry to map view. Mini-map gave users a glimpse inside the map view.

Enhancement #2

Making maps more engaging

To make maps engaging, we wanted to give travelers a sense of what was around them, using their current location or their booked hotel.

Once travelers entered the map view, they could also see the nearby view in 3D.

Leveraged traveler data to show activities near them.

A 3D view to match with real world.

Enhancement #3

Narrow down on activity choices

We tried two variations.

First, used generic filters provided by the API to create a cohesive experience irrespective of device type, mobile or desktop.

Generic filter offered a broad range of options to narrow down on an activity.

Second, used quick filters to bring up the filters requested by travelers in the user study: popularity, proximity, and price.

Frequently used filters were placed outside the generic filter for easy reach.

Enhancement #4

Show distance on map card

Travelers wanted to know how far they need to travel for an activity. The task was to show the distance from their current location or booked hotel.

However, just before this iteration a change in the design system led to complications on the map card.

Change in the design system led to complications on the map card.

Re-designed the map card by -

  • exploring different variations to reduce card height
  • keeping in mind the hierarchy of the elements
  • trying translations in languages which could lead to longer text

Thus, showed distance of an activity from user's location, or booked hotel, by redesigning the map card.

Redesigned map-card keeping in mind the hierarchy of all elements.

Enhancement #5

Distinguish between activity pins

We wanted to explore use of icons on the map pins, as done by competitors and requested by some users.

Exploration of icons for activity type.

Did an audit of the activity categories and the icons already available in the design system. Next task was to create the missing icons.

Some icons were available in design system.

Unfortunately, the pandemic happened, and things came to a surprise halt.

Learnings

The learnings were -

  • Importance of user studies along with AB testing to build user-centric products.
  • Need to over-communicate across teams to create a cohesive experience.
  • Addition of a new element will add to the complexity, and simplification is a never-ending process.
